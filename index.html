🌍 Core Definitions & Scope
Ecology is the study of the interactions between living organisms, including humans, animals and the overall environment.
↑ click the box to reveal
German scientist Ernst Haeckel defined ecology in 1866 as the relationship between living organisms and the environment.
The largest scale of ecological organization is the biosphere: the total sum of ecosystems on the planet.
The biological world is organized into a nested hierarchy ranging from genes → cells → tissues → organs → organisms → species → populations → communities → ecosystems → biomes → biosphere.
Ecological hierarchy is grouped into four levels: individual, population, community, and ecosystem level.
The individual level is the first level of ecological hierarchy.
Ecology is divided into autecology (individual species) and synecology (communities).
Evolutionary concepts relating to adaptation and natural selection are cornerstones of modern ecological theory.
🔄 Panarchy & Resilience
A panarchy exhibits non-linear behaviors — effect and cause are disproportionate.
A panarchy is a set of nested adaptive cycles across time and space.
Panarchy grew out of resilience and hierarchy theory.
Panarchy identifies four basic stages of ecosystems: exploitation (r), conservation (K), release (omega), and reorganization (alpha).
A thing is said to be resilient if it has the capacity to withstand or recover from disturbance.
The 2025 LA wildfires in areas like Hollywood Hills and Pacific Palisades serve as a classic example of panarchy.
🌿 Biodiversity & Habitat
Biodiversity includes species diversity, ecosystem diversity, and genetic diversity.
The total number of species on earth is estimated to be about 7 million as estimated by Robert May.
Plants constitute more than 70% of all species recorded; animals constitute less than 22%; insects constitute more than 70% of all animal species.
The habitat of a species describes the environment over which a species is known to occur and the type of community that forms as a result.
Ontogenetic niche shift (ONS) is an ecological phenomenon where an organism changes its diet or habitat during its ontogeny (development).
Fish commonly undergo ontogenetic habitat shifts — they change habitat over their life span as they grow in size.
A montane ecosystem is one found in mountains.
🐾 Niche, Ecotope & Niche Construction
The fundamental niche is the set of environmental conditions under which a species is able to persist. The realized niche is the set of environmental PLUS ecological conditions under which a species persists.
The competitive exclusion principle states that two species cannot coexist indefinitely by living off the same limiting resource.
The habitat plus the niche is called the ecotope.
Classic examples of niche construction include beavers building dams, birds building nests, and worms altering soil structure.
The "human climate niche" averages 11–15°C and humans have been thriving in it for thousands of years.
As of 2024, more than 600 million people are already living outside the crucial "climate niche."
Beavers are called ecosystem engineers because they create, modify, and maintain habitat and ecosystems.
Beavers do not live in the dams but in the deep pond in structures called lodges.
🏗 Ecosystem Engineering
The concept of ecosystem engineering was introduced by Jones et al. (1994).
Autogenic engineers change the environment via their own physical structures (e.g., corals creating reefs).
Allogenic engineers transform living or non-living materials from one physical state to another (e.g., beavers building dams, earthworms restructuring soil).
Termite mounds maintain a constant internal temperature through the design of air-conditioning chimneys.
The process of ecosystem engineering relates to niche construction, but ecosystem engineering relates only to physical modifications of habitat, whereas niche construction also considers evolutionary implications.
🌲 Biomes & Biosphere
Biomes are the largest geographic biotic unit — a major community of plants and animals with similar life forms. Types include: aquatic, grassland, forest, desert, tundra (treeless), and taiga (coniferous forest).
The total human gut microbiome weighs approximately 1 to 2 kilograms in an average adult.
Coral reefs are formed of coral polyps held together by calcium carbonate.
Marine microbes drive chemical reactions that contribute to Earth's habitability and are responsible for the cycling of elements such as carbon, oxygen, nitrogen, phosphorus, sulfur, minerals, salts and trace gases.
👥 Population Ecology
A population consists of individuals of the same species that live, interact, and migrate through the same niche and habitat.
The Malthusian law states human population increases geometrically (exponentially), while food production increases only arithmetically (linearly).
Simplified population models start with four variables: death, birth, immigration, and emigration.
The Malthusian growth model was transformed into the logistic equation by Pierre Verhulst.
The exponential growth formula is: r = ln(pn/p0)/n, where r is the exponential rate of growth.
In the logistic equation, r is the maximum per-capita rate of change (intrinsic rate of growth) and α is the crowding coefficient, which represents the reduction in population growth rate per individual added.
In metapopulation terminology, migrating individuals are classed as emigrants (leaving a region) or immigrants (entering a region), and sites are classed as sources or sinks.
The concept of metapopulations was defined by Levins in 1969 as "a population of populations which go extinct locally and recolonize."
Source patches are productive sites that generate a seasonal supply of juveniles that migrate to other patch locations. Sink patches are unproductive sites that only receive migrants.
Dispersal is the one-way permanent movement of individuals from their birth population into another population (distinguished from migration which is seasonal/return).
When an island is first colonized, the population experiences density-independent forces of natural selection, called r-selection. As the population approaches carrying capacity, it experiences density-dependent forces, called K-selection.
An r-selected species has high birth rates, low levels of parental investment, and high rates of mortality before maturity. Humans and elephants are examples of K-selected species.
🍽 Food Webs & Trophic Levels
A food web is the archetypal ecological network.
Plants capture solar energy and synthesize simple sugars during photosynthesis.
The simplified linear feeding pathway from bottom trophic species to top consumer is called the food chain.
Species are broadly categorized as autotrophs (primary producers), heterotrophs (consumers), and detritivores (decomposers).
Autotrophs produce their own food by photosynthesis or chemosynthesis. Key enzymes involved are RuBisCO and carbonic anhydrase.
Heterotrophs can be sub-divided into primary consumers (strict herbivores), secondary consumers (carnivores feeding on herbivores), and tertiary consumers (predators feeding on a mix of herbivores and predators).
Omnivores do not fit neatly into a functional category — they are considered to have greater functional influence as predators because they are relatively inefficient at grazing.
A keystone species is connected to a disproportionately large number of other species in the food web and has lower levels of biomass relative to the importance of its role.
Sea otters (Enhydra lutris) limit the density of sea urchins that feed on kelp. Hunting of sea otters led indirectly to the extinction of the Steller's sea cow (Hydrodamalis gigas).
Sloth bears are a keystone species in India because their food, consisting mostly of termites, fruits, and tubers, helps preserve the health of a forest.
Trophic cascades are ecological phenomena when a change in population of a species causes a ripple effect throughout the food chain.
In India, vulture populations dwindled up to 95% due to the anti-inflammatory drug diclofenac administered to cattle, leading to a rabies outbreak through rising feral dog populations.
Insectivores are animals, primarily small mammals like shrews, moles, and hedgehogs, that subsist mainly on insects, larvae, and other invertebrates.
Zooplankton are small aquatic microorganisms including crustaceans, rotifers, open water insect larvae and aquatic mites. They may be herbivores, carnivores, or omnivores.
🤝 Species Interactions & Coevolution
If there is a physical connection between host and associate, the relationship is called symbiosis.
Approximately 60% of all plants have a symbiotic relationship with Arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi living in their roots, forming an exchange network of phosphates, carbohydrates for mineral nutrients.
When the associate benefits while the host suffers, the relationship is called parasitism.
Indirect mutualisms occur where organisms live apart. This is called commensalism when many others receive benefits at no cost or harm to the provider (e.g., trees supplying oxygen).
The Red Queen Hypothesis posits that parasites track down and specialize on the locally common genetic defense systems of their host, driving evolution of sexual reproduction.
Relationships between species that are mutually or reciprocally beneficial are called mutualisms. Examples include fungus-growing ants, bacteria in insect guts, fig wasp-yucca moth pollination, lichens (fungi + algae), and corals with algae.
Allelopathy is the phenomenon where plants like Banyan, Eucalyptus, and Black walnut suppress growth of other plants.
Phenotype refers to an individual's observable traits, such as height, eye color, and blood type.
🗺 Biogeography & Distribution
Biogeography is an amalgamation of biology and geography — the comparative study of geographic distribution of organisms and corresponding evolution of their traits in space and time.
In the r/K-selection model, r is the intrinsic rate of natural increase and K is the carrying capacity of a population.
Glass sponges are considered the oldest animals on Earth, with scientists estimating they can live for more than 10,000 years, possibly 15,000 years maximum.
The giant tortoise is known for its long lifespan, with some individuals living over 150 years. They are found on Aldabra Atoll / Galápagos Islands.
🧠 Behavioral & Cognitive Ecology
Behavioral ecology is the study of an organism's behavior in its environment and its ecological and evolutionary implications.
Ethology is the study of observable movement or behavior in animals.
Circadian rhythms are physical, mental, and behavioral changes that follow a 24-hour cycle. Chronobiology is the study of circadian rhythms.
Young sunflowers display heliotropism, moving their heads from east to west to follow the sun's path. This movement is driven by circadian rhythms and differential stem growth.
Crows and parrots recognizing and remembering individual human faces is a premier example of cognitive ecology.
Social-ecological behaviors are notable in social insects, slime molds, social spiders, human society, and naked mole-rats where eusocialism has evolved.
The Socratea exorrhiza, or "walking palm", found in South American rainforests, can "walk" across the forest floor using stilt roots that grow toward better sunlight.
🌡 Environmental Physics & Climate
The Earth was formed approximately 4.5 billion years ago. Its atmosphere first transformed from hydrogen to mostly methane and ammonia.
The transition to an oxygen-dominant atmosphere (the Great Oxidation) did not begin until approximately 2.4–2.3 billion years ago.
The earliest organisms were likely anaerobic methanogen microbes that converted atmospheric hydrogen into methane.
Average temperature on Earth = 15°C. Average temperature on Venus = 464°C due to CO₂ atmosphere. Average temperature on Moon ranges from −183°C to 106°C.
Dinosaurs lived for 200 million years and got eliminated 65 million years ago due to a huge asteroid or comet impact.
Methane is more than 28 times as potent as carbon dioxide at trapping heat in the atmosphere.
The IPCC (1996) estimated global methane emission rate from paddy fields at 60 Tg/yr, with a range of 20 to 100 Tg/yr.
A single cow produces between 154 to 264 pounds of methane gas per year.
The Earth's oceans hold 40,000 Gt of carbon; vegetation and soil hold 2070 Gt; fossil fuel emissions are 6.3 Gt carbon per year.
In the Oligocene (~25–32 million years ago), grasses evolved C4 photosynthesis in response to the drop in atmospheric CO₂ below 550 ppm. Present CO₂ level = 421 ppm.
Atmospheric pressure on earth is about 14.7 pounds per square inch.
Diffusion of CO₂ and O₂ is approximately 10,000 times slower in water than in air. Hypoxic environments have O₂ concentration below 2 mg/liter.
CO₂ concentration in water is just 10 ppm while in air it is 421 ppm.
Plants convert CO₂ into biomass and emit oxygen. By approximately 350 million years ago (end of Devonian period), photosynthesis had brought atmospheric oxygen above 17%, allowing combustion to occur.
Environmentally triggered germination of seeds (e.g., after fire) is called serotiny. Example: Pinus halepensis cannot germinate until seeds have lived through a fire.
Soil is the chief organizing centre of most ecosystem functions.
Six major elements forming all biological macromolecules: H, C, N, O, S, P (hydrogen, carbon, nitrogen, oxygen, sulfur, and phosphorus).
The father of Physical Geography and Earlier Ecology was Alexander von Humboldt (1769–1859), who first recognized ecological gradients.
The only known animal to completely lack mitochondrial DNA and not require oxygen is Henneguya salminicola.
Wind running over a lake surface creates turbulence, mixing the water column and creating thermally layered zones. Dissolved oxygen levels in water increase due to turbulence.
The air expands and moisture condenses as winds increase in elevation — this is called orographic lift and can cause precipitation.
Salt water plants (halophytes) have specialized adaptations to live in estuarine, brackish, or oceanic environments, including special organs for shedding salt and osmoregulating their internal NaCl concentrations.
Gravitational forces provide directional cues for plant and fungal growth, a phenomenon called gravitropism.
🧬 Molecular Ecology
Molecular ecological research became more feasible with the development of the polymerase chain reaction (PCR).
The Indian gene pool is composed of four ancestral genetic components: ANI (Ancestral North Indian), ASI (Ancestral South Indian), ATB (Ancestral Tibeto-Burman), and AAA (Ancestral Austro-Asiatic).
The proportion of ANI ancestry in most Indian groups ranges from 39–71%. ANI are genetically closer to Middle Easterners, Central Asians, and Europeans.
🦎 Complexity, Chaos & Disturbance
Complexity in ecology is of at least six distinct types: spatial, temporal, structural, process, behavioral, and geometric.
Ecologists sort fluctuations into three broad categories: "regular" (fluctuating around a stable equilibrium), "random" (impossible to predict), and "chaotic" (predictable in short term but not long term).
The metaphor of the butterfly effect was first articulated by mathematician Edward Lorenz, a pioneer of chaos theory.
A disturbance is any process that removes biomass from a community, such as fire, flood, drought, cyclones, or predation.
Technoecosystems are a more recent addition to ecosystem ecology — affected by or primarily the result of human activity.
Edward O. Wilson predicted in 1992 that the 21st century will be the era of restoration in ecology.
China reported its lowest birth rate since 1949 in 2025, with births plunging to 7.92 million, a 17% decline from 9.54 million in 2024.
The Chelyabinsk, Russia meteorite event occurred on February 15, 2013. The shock wave injured around 1,500 people and shattered more than 3,600 windows.
China's "War on Sparrows" (Four Pests Campaign) targeted rats, flies, mosquitoes, and sparrows — resulting in a locust plague that killed 45 million people (1958–1962).
In the Maldives, the Maldivian president held the world's first underwater cabinet meeting in October 2009 as a cry for help over rising sea levels. Maldives is expected to be entirely submerged by 2100.

Ecology [click to expand]
Ecology is the study of the interactions between living organisms, including humans, animals and the overall environment. It tries to understand organisms at the individual, population, community, ecosystem, and biosphere level and how these live and deal with each other.
TOPIC: Core Definitions
Ecosystem [click to expand]
Ecosystems involve biological, chemical, and physical feedback mechanisms. They include different processes acting on both living (biotic) and non-living (abiotic) components of Earth. Ecosystems sustain life and perform life-supporting functions including biomass production, climate regulation, global biogeochemical cycles, water filtration, and soil formation. They are broadly categorized as terrestrial, freshwater, atmospheric, or marine.
TOPIC: Ecosystems
Ecological Niche [click to expand]
Niche is the set of biotic and abiotic conditions in which a species is able to persist and maintain stable population sizes. It is subdivided into two: (1) The fundamental niche — the set of environmental conditions under which a species can persist. (2) The realized niche — the set of environmental PLUS ecological conditions under which a species persists.
TOPIC: Niche
Panarchy [click to expand]
Panarchy is a concept that grew out of resilience and hierarchy theory. It is a useful tool for understanding uncertainty and the unforeseen in an era of rapid environmental change. A panarchy is a set of nested (across time and space) adaptive cycles that emphasizes the inevitable and inherent dynamics of living systems: growth, conservation, release, and reorganization. It exhibits non-linear behaviors — effect and cause are disproportionate.
TOPIC: Panarchy
Resilience (ecological) [click to expand]
A thing is said to be resilient if it has the capacity to withstand or recover from disturbance. In an ecological sense, resilience describes the persistence of ecosystem structure and function in the face of changing conditions.
TOPIC: Resilience
Biodiversity [click to expand]
Biodiversity includes the diversity of life from genes to ecosystems and covers every level of biological organization. It includes species diversity, ecosystem diversity, and genetic diversity. Scientists are interested in how this diversity affects the complex ecological processes operating at and among these respective levels.
TOPIC: Biodiversity
Keystone Species [click to expand]
A keystone species is a species that is connected to a disproportionately large number of other species in the food web. It has lower levels of biomass in the trophic pyramid relative to the importance of its role. The loss of a keystone species results in dramatic trophic cascades and can cause extinction of other species. Example: sea otters limit sea urchin populations that feed on kelp.
TOPIC: Trophic Dynamics
Metapopulation [click to expand]
A metapopulation is a "population of populations" distributed in discrete habitat patches that are linked by occasional dispersal. It is a group of spatially separated populations of the same species that interact at some level. The concept was defined by Levins in 1969. The ecology of metapopulations is a dynamic process of extinction and colonization.
TOPIC: Population Ecology
Niche Construction [click to expand]
Niche construction is the evolutionary and ecological process where organisms modify their local environments, creating new selection pressures that influence their own evolution and that of other species. It is a fundamental, nearly universal phenomenon, ranging from animals building nests and dams to plants altering soil nutrients and microbes changing atmospheric conditions. It highlights a two-way interaction between organisms and their environment.
TOPIC: Niche Construction
Ecosystem Engineering [click to expand]
Ecosystem engineering refers to the process where organisms directly or indirectly modulate the availability of resources to other species by causing physical state changes in biotic or abiotic materials. These organisms create, modify, maintain, or destroy habitats, significantly affecting community structure and biodiversity. Key examples: beavers damming streams, corals building reefs, earthworms altering soil. Two types: autogenic (via own structure) and allogenic (transforming materials).
TOPIC: Ecosystem Engineering
Trophic Cascade [click to expand]
Trophic cascades are ecological phenomena that occur when there is a change in the population of a particular species, which then causes a ripple effect throughout the food chain, impacting other species in either a positive or negative manner. Classic example: removal of wolves from Yellowstone caused deer populations to explode and over-graze vegetation.
TOPIC: Trophic Dynamics
Biome [click to expand]
Biomes are the largest geographic biotic units — major communities of plants and animals with similar life forms and environmental conditions. Types include: aquatic, grassland, forest, desert, tundra (treeless), and taiga (coniferous forest). Biomes are larger units of organization that categorize regions of Earth's ecosystems mainly according to the structure and composition of vegetation.
TOPIC: Biomes
Ecotope [click to expand]
The habitat plus the niche is called the ecotope. When similarly adapted species overlap geographically, closer inspection reveals subtle ecological differences in their habitat or dietary requirements.
TOPIC: Habitat & Niche
Biogeography [click to expand]
Biogeography is an amalgamation of biology and geography — the comparative study of the geographic distribution of organisms and the corresponding evolution of their traits in space and time. Biogeographical patterns result from ecological processes that influence range distributions, such as migration and dispersal, and from historical processes that split populations or species into different areas.
TOPIC: Biogeography
Ontogenetic Niche Shift (ONS) [click to expand]
Ontogenetic Niche Shift (ONS) is an ecological phenomenon where an organism (usually an animal) changes its diet or habitat during its ontogeny (development). During this shift, the ecological niche of an individual changes its breadth and position. Common example: fish change habitat over their lifespan as they grow in size; habitats for spawning and juvenile growth often differ from habitats used by adults.
TOPIC: Habitat & Niche
Behavioural Ecology [click to expand]
Behavioral ecology is the study of an organism's behavior in its environment and its ecological and evolutionary implications. Ethology is the study of observable movement or behavior in animals. All organisms can exhibit behaviors — even plants express complex behavior including memory and communication. Circadian rhythms are physical, mental, and behavioral changes that follow a 24-hour cycle; Chronobiology is the study of circadian rhythms.
TOPIC: Behavioural Ecology
Community Ecology [click to expand]
Community ecology is the study of the interactions among a collection of species that inhabit the same geographic area. Community ecologists study the patterns and processes for two or more interacting species. Research might measure species diversity in grasslands in relation to soil fertility, or analyze predator-prey dynamics, competition among similar plant species, or mutualistic interactions between crabs and corals.
TOPIC: Types of Ecology
Serotiny [click to expand]
Serotiny is the environmentally triggered germination of seeds following fire or exposure to fire-related compounds. For example, Pinus halepensis cannot germinate until after its seeds have lived through a fire or been exposed to certain compounds from smoke. Fire plays a major role in the persistence and resilience of ecosystems.
TOPIC: Fire Ecology
Mutualism [click to expand]
Mutualism refers to relationships between species that are mutually or reciprocally beneficial. Examples include fungus-growing ants (agricultural symbiosis), bacteria in insect guts, the fig wasp and yucca moth pollination complex, lichens (fungi + photosynthetic algae), and corals with photosynthetic algae.
TOPIC: Coevolution
Holism (in ecology) [click to expand]
Holism means the overall patterns of a whole functional system, such as an ecosystem, cannot be predicted or understood by a simple summation of the parts. New properties emerge because the components interact. For example, mutualism, commensalism, and antagonism — their combined effect is unpredictable by summation.
TOPIC: Complexity

📐 Exponential Growth Rate Formula
r = ln(pn / p0) / n
r = exponential rate of growth  |  ln = natural logarithm  |  pn = end period population  |  p0 = beginning period population  |  n = number of years
📐 Rate of Population Change (Island Model)
dN(t)/dt = bN(t) – dN(t) = (b–d)N(t) = rN(t)
N = total individuals  |  b = per capita birth rate  |  d = per capita death rate  |  r = per capita rate of population change
📐 Logistic Equation (Pierre Verhulst)
dN(t)/dt = rN(t) – αN(t)² ≡ rN(t)(K–N(t))/K
r = intrinsic rate of growth (max per-capita rate of change)  |  α = crowding coefficient  |  K = carrying capacity  |  Equilibrium when dN(t)/dt = 0
⚗️ Photosynthesis Reactions
Light: 2H₂O + 2NADP⁺ + 3ADP + 3Pi → O₂ + 2NADPH + 3ATP
Dark: 3CO₂ + 6NADPH + 5H₂O + 9ATP → G3P + 2H⁺ + 6NADP⁺ + 9ADP + 8Pi
NADPH = Nicotinamide Adenine Dinucleotide Phosphate
⚗️ Early Atmosphere Reactions
Methanogen: 4H₂ + CO₂ → CH₄ + 2H₂O
Anoxygenic photosynthesis: 2H₂S + CO₂ + hv → CH₂O + H₂O + 2S
Algae CO₂ equilibrium: CO₂ + H₂O ⟷ HCO₃⁻ + H⁺
