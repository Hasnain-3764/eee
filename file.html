📖 Definitions — All Likely Exam Terms

Exam tip: Prof may ask definition of any term from anywhere in the slides. These are the most quotable, precise definitions from the lectures.
Biodiversity PPT
Biodiversity
The variety and differences among living organisms from all sources, including terrestrial, marine, and other aquatic ecosystems and the ecological complexes of which they are a part. Biologists define it as the "totality of genes, species, and ecosystems of a region." It is virtually synonymous with "Life on Earth."
Biodiversity PPT
Alpha Diversity
Within community diversity. Refers to the diversity of organisms sharing the same Community/Habitat.
Biodiversity PPT
Beta Diversity
Between community diversity. Refers to the diversity of organisms sharing two habitats.
Biodiversity PPT
Gamma Diversity
Diversity of the habitat over the total landscape or geographical area.
Biodiversity PPT
Species Diversity
An index that incorporates the number of species in an area and their relative abundance (Simpson Index). More useful than species richness (Shannon Wiener Index).
Biodiversity PPT
Genetic Diversity
A level of biodiversity that refers to the total number of genetic characteristics in the genetic makeup of a species — i.e., variation within species (e.g., different breeds of dogs, rose flowers).
Biodiversity PPT
Biodiversity Hotspot
A biogeography region with a significant reservoir of biodiversity that is threatened with destruction. An area is a hotspot if it contains at least 1,500 species of vascular plants found nowhere else (endemic) AND has lost at least 70% of its original native vegetation.
Biodiversity PPT
Threatened Species
Any species (animals, plants, fungi, etc.) that is vulnerable to endangerment in the near future. Example: Bengal tigers.
Biodiversity PPT
Extinct Species
Species not found after searches of known or likely areas where they may occur. May be locally, regionally, or globally extinct. Examples: Quagga, Dodo.
Biodiversity PPT
Endemic Species
Species found only in some particular areas, usually isolated by natural or geographical barriers. Example: Asiatic lion.
Biodiversity PPT
Endangered Species
Species in danger of extinction. Survival is difficult if negative factors causing decline continue to operate. Example: Indian gharial. (IUCN: in imminent danger of extinction and whose survival is unlikely if decline factors continue.)
Biodiversity PPT
Conservation
The management of human use of the biosphere so that it may yield the greatest sustainable benefit to the present generation while maintaining its potential to meet the needs and aspirations of future generations.
Biodiversity PPT
In-situ Conservation
Conservation of species in its natural ecosystem or even in man-made ecosystems. Emphasizes protection of total ecosystems through a network of protected areas (Biosphere reserves, National Parks, Wildlife Sanctuaries).
Biodiversity PPT
Ex-situ Conservation
Conservation of components of biological diversity (sample of genetic diversity, particularly of endangered species) outside their natural habitats. E.g., Zoos, Botanical Gardens, Aquaria, DNA banks, Seed banks, Gene banks.
Biodiversity PPT
Protected Area
An area of land and/or sea specially dedicated to the protection and maintenance of biological diversity and managed through legal and effective means.
Biodiversity PPT
Biosystems Engineering
An integrated science that combines engineering design and analysis with biological sciences to address agricultural, environmental, food safety, and ecological concerns across the production-processing system from farm and production to consumers.
Biodiversity PPT
Biological Diversity Act, 2002
Indian legislation aimed at conserving biological resources, promoting sustainable use, and ensuring fair sharing of benefits. Enacted to meet India's obligations under the UN CBD. Mandates NBA, SBBs, and BMCs. (Notably updated in 2023.)
Env. Challenges PPT
Greenwashing
The deceptive marketing tactic of portraying a company, product, or service as more environmentally friendly or sustainable than it actually is. Capitalizes on consumer demand for green products through vague claims, misleading labeling, or hidden tradeoffs.
Env. Challenges PPT
Bioinvasion
The intentional or accidental introduction of organisms in regions where they are not native. Example: Red fire ants introduced from Paraguay/Brazil to Alabama (USA) in 1957 via lumber shipments.
Env. Challenges PPT
Global Warming
The increasing average global air temperature, caused mainly by the accumulation of greenhouse gases (primarily CO₂, methane, nitrous oxide) in the atmosphere that act like the glass in a greenhouse, holding heat from the sun close to the earth.
Env. Challenges PPT
Environmental Kuznets Curve (EKC)
The concept that the relationship between economic development and environmental pollution is curvilinear (inverted U-shape): environmental degradation increases during early economic development, peaks at a threshold, then declines as the country becomes wealthier.
Env. Challenges PPT
Multiple Chemical Sensitivity (MCS)
Also known as environmental illness. A condition where individuals experience adverse reactions when exposed to low levels of chemicals found in everyday substances (exhaust, paint, fragrances, synthetic building materials, petrochemical-based products).
Env. Challenges PPT
Planned Obsolescence
Manufacturers design products intended to become obsolete so consumers continually discard used products and buy replacements. Industry profits at the expense of the environment (conflict perspective).
Env. Challenges PPT
Environmental Justice / Environmental Racism
The tendency for marginalized populations and communities to disproportionately experience adversity due to environmental problems (e.g., vehicular pollution, industrial waste near poor communities).
Env. Challenges PPT
Desertification
Land degradation process. Approximately 29.3–30% of India's total geographical area (~96–97 million hectares) is undergoing land degradation or desertification; primary driver is water erosion (80%), followed by vegetation degradation and wind erosion.
Carbon PPT
Carbon Footprint
The amount of carbon dioxide (CO₂) emissions associated with an individual, event, organization, service, or product. Reducing carbon footprints can be achieved by replacing coal with gas/oil, using solar/wind energy, hydrogen fuel cells, or cleaner electric energy.
Carbon PPT
Quad (Unit of Energy)
A unit of energy equal to 10¹⁵ (one short-scale quadrillion) BTU, or 1.055×10¹⁸ joules (1.055 exajoules or EJ) in SI units. Reference: Gilbert M. Masters, "Introduction to Environmental Engineering and Science," Prentice Hall, 1991.
Biodiversity PPT
Entoremediation
Environmentally sustainable, cost-effective natural process where insect larvae (mealworms, wax worms) consume and biodegrade plastics like polystyrene and polyethylene using microbes in their guts, converting them into organic, non-toxic compost.
Env. Challenges PPT
Acid Rain
Formed when air pollutants (sulfur dioxide, nitrogen oxide) mix with precipitation. Polluted rain, snow, and fog contaminate crops, forests, lakes, rivers, and oceans. Example: All fish have died in a third of the lakes in New York's Adirondack Mountains due to acid rain.
Biodiversity PPT
National Biodiversity Authority (NBA)
A statutory, autonomous body established in 2003 under the Biological Diversity Act, 2002 to implement the act's provisions, focusing on conservation, sustainable use, and fair benefit-sharing. Headquartered in Chennai.
Env. Challenges PPT
EIA (Environmental Impact Assessment)
Mandatory under the Environmental Protection Act, 1986 for 29 categories of developmental activities involving investments of Rs. 50 crores and above. Governing instrument: EIA Notification, 2006, for green clearance for industry establishment or expansion.

🌿 Biodiversity — Core Facts

  • Biodiversity found on Earth is the product of nearly 3.5 billion years of evolution.
  • Biologists define biodiversity as the totality of genes, species, and ecosystems of a region.
  • Species diversity is measured using the Simpson Index (incorporates number and relative abundance).
  • Species richness is measured using the Shannon Wiener Index.
  • About 80,000 edible plants exist, and about 90% of present-day food crops have been domesticated from wild.
  • About 75% of the world's population depends on plants and plant extracts for drugs and medicines.
  • India's position in plant diversity is 10th in the world and 4th in Asia.
  • About 2.1 million species have been identified till date.
  • A biodiversity hotspot must contain at least 1,500 species of vascular plants found nowhere else (endemic) and have lost at least 70% of its original native vegetation.
  • An area is also designated as a hotspot when it contains at least 0.5% of plant species as endemic.
  • There are 25 such hotspots of biodiversity on a global level, out of which two are present in India.
  • The two biodiversity hotspots in India are Indo-Burma (Eastern Himalayas) and Western Ghats & Sri Lanka.
  • These hotspots cover less than 2% of the world's land area but have about 50% of terrestrial biodiversity.
  • The Western Ghats & Sri Lanka hotspot is also known as the "Sahyadri Hills".
  • The original extent of the Western Ghats hotspot was about 1,82,500 sq km, but only 12,445 sq km (6.8%) is now in pristine condition.
  • Indo-Burma hotspot includes all of Cambodia, Vietnam, and Laos, and nearly entire areas of Thailand, Myanmar, and Bhutan.
  • Criteria for hotspot: No. of Endemic Species and Degree of threat measured in terms of Habitat Loss.
  • Habitat loss and degradation affect 89% of all threatened birds, 83% of mammals, and 91% of all threatened plants (IUCN, 2000).
  • The Convention on Biological Diversity was held in Rio de Janeiro in June, 1992.
  • India has 11 major biosphere reserves, 80 National parks, and 420 wildlife sanctuaries, covering 4% of geographic area.
  • The Jim Corbett National Park was the 1st national park established in India.
  • Out of about 47,000 species of plants in India, 7,000 are endemic.
  • India contains 172 species of animals considered globally threatened by IUCN, which is 2.9% of the world's total threatened species.
  • These 172 threatened species include 53 mammals, 69 birds, 23 reptiles, and 3 amphibians.
  • About 3,000–4,000 higher plants may be under high degree of threat in India.
  • Indian subcontinent has about 62% endemic flora, restricted mainly to Himalayas, Khasi Hills, and Western Ghats.
  • About 62% amphibians and 50% lizards are endemic to Western Ghats.
  • About 81,000 species of animals are found in India.
  • There are more than 1,500 Botanical Gardens in the world containing more than 80,000 species.
  • There are more than 800 zoos around the world with about 3,000 species of mammals, birds, reptiles, and amphibians.
  • NBPGR stands for National Bureau of Plant Genetic Resources, located in New Delhi.
  • In NBPGR, seeds are preserved by cryo-preservation at -196°C in Liquid Nitrogen.
  • The Wild Life (Protection) Act, 1972 provides a comprehensive legal framework for protection of wild animals, birds, and plants, covering the entire country.
  • The Wildlife (Protection) Amendment Act, 2022 streamlined the original six schedules into four distinct categories.
  • In the Wildlife schedules, Schedule I offers highest protection/endangered species, Schedule II offers lesser protection/general, Schedule III is for protected plants, and Schedule IV is for CITES-listed specimens.
  • The Biological Diversity Act was enacted in 2002 and notably updated in 2023.
  • The NBA (National Biodiversity Authority) was established in 2003 and is headquartered in Chennai.
  • The National Medicinal Plants Board (NMPB) was established in November 2000 under the Ministry of AYUSH.
  • De-extinction: revival of species from 12,000 years ago through genetic engineering.
  • Of 80,000 known edible plants, we depend on 20 species to provide 90% of global food supply; corn, rice, and wheat alone provide 50%.
  • 40% of modern medicines come from plants/animals/fungi, and 95% of known plant species have never been tested for medicinal value.
  • Engineers Ireland declared a climate and biodiversity emergency in March 2020.
  • Engineers Ireland has 25,000 members.
  • Green engineering of USEPA is the design, commercialization, and use of processes and products that minimize pollution, promote sustainability, and protect human health.

🦋 Insects & Ecology

  • Mosquito larvae consume pollutants from water and mosquitoes play a role in pollinating certain plants.
  • House flies are strongly suspected of transmitting at least 65 diseases to humans.
  • The insect larvae that can consume plastics include Tenebrio molitor (yellow mealworm), Zophobas atratus (superworm), and Galleria mellonella (wax worm).
  • Key dung beetle types are rollers, tunnelers, and dwellers.

🌍 Environmental Challenges — Facts

  • The UN has adopted international frameworks for three key issues encoded as the "triple planetary crises".
  • The share of renewables in global electricity generation reached 34.3% in 2026.
  • India is the world's third-largest renewable energy (RE) market.
  • India's installed renewable energy capacity reached 274.68 GW by March 2026, a 3.59-fold increase since 2014.
  • India's solar capacity is 150.26 GW and wind capacity is 56.09 GW.
  • India added a record 55.29 GW of non-fossil capacity in FY 2025–26, aiming for 500 GW by 2030.
  • More than 1 billion people lack access to clean water.
  • Air pollution kills about 3 million people a year.
  • About half of the US population lives in areas with unhealthy levels of air pollution.
  • Indoor smoke from cooking increases risk of pneumonia, chronic respiratory disease, asthma, cataract, tuberculosis, and lung cancer, responsible for up to 1.6 million deaths a year (WHO 2010).
  • The ozone layer is located in the stratosphere, about 20–25 km above the Earth.
  • The ozone layer has been weakened by chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) and HCCs used in refrigerators, air conditioners, spray cans. The fourth generation of synthetic refrigerants are HFOs (Hydrofluoroolefins).
  • Radioactive plutonium has a half-life of 24,000 years.
  • In 1960, each U.S. citizen generated 2.7 pounds of garbage per day; this rose to 3.7 pounds in 1980, 4.3 pounds in 2009, and about 5 pounds in 2017.
  • MSW (Municipal Solid Waste) in India is 62 million tonnes (mT) per annum.
  • In 2011, 1,287 hazardous waste sites (Superfund sites) were on the National Priority List in the US.
  • About 3 million tons of toxic chemicals are released into the environment each year.
  • The 12th Report on Carcinogens lists 240 chemical substances as "known" or "reasonably anticipated" human carcinogens.
  • There are an estimated 8.7 million species of life on earth; 1.3 million have been named and catalogued.
  • About 30% of the world's surface is land.
  • In 2009, oil and gas industries spent $175 million on lobbying—nearly eight times what pro-environmental groups spent.
  • The world's population reached the 8 billion mark in 2022.
  • Urbanisation in India is about 34%, against 56% world average.
  • The CO₂ level in the atmosphere in 2026 is approximately 430 ppm.
  • Rise of Earth's surface temperature by 1.5°C over the 1800s baseline.
  • Climate change kills an estimated 30,000 people per year, mostly in the developing world.
  • Ecosystem services have an estimated global value exceeding $125 trillion per year.
  • PCBs (polychlorinated biphenyls), i.e., dioxins, from the Southern Hemisphere have been found in the Arctic.
  • Red fire ants came from Paraguay and Brazil on shiploads of lumber to Mobile, Alabama in 1957.
  • India is the world's 6th largest producer of chemicals; China, the United States, and Germany take the lead.
  • There are more than 840,000 dams worldwide that provide water and electricity.
  • Approximately 29.3–30% of India's total geographical area is undergoing land degradation, highest in Rajasthan, Maharashtra, Gujarat, Karnataka, and Jammu & Kashmir. Water erosion is primary driver (80%).
  • EIA has been made mandatory for 29 categories of developmental activities involving investments of Rs. 50 crores and above.
  • The governing instrument for EIA in India is the EIA Notification, 2006.
  • Pinkwashing uses the color pink and pink ribbons to market companies as helping fight breast cancer.

🌡️ Carbon & Energy

  • Replacing coal with gas or oil would reduce the carbon dioxide emission (carbon footprints).
  • Solar energy and wind energy have almost zero carbon dioxide emissions.
  • A quad = 10¹⁵ BTU = 1.055×10¹⁸ joules = 1.055 exajoules (EJ).
  • Hydroelectric power accounts for 6.2% of global energy; considered clean and renewable but criticized for affecting natural habitats.
  • The most common source of global energy is fossil fuels (petroleum, coal, natural gas).
  • Reference book for carbon numericals: Introduction to Environmental Engineering and Science by Gilbert M. Masters, Prentice Hall of India, 1991.

⚖️ Laws, Conventions & Bodies

  • The Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD) was established in 1992 to promote conservation, sustainable use, and equitable sharing of genetic resources.
  • The Aichi Targets (2011–2020) had 20 goals to curb biodiversity loss.
  • The Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework (KMGBF) was adopted at COP 15 in 2022, comprising 4 major goals and 23 urgent targets for 2030.
  • KMGBF sets a global mission to halt and reverse biodiversity loss by 2030, with ultimate vision of humans living in harmony with nature by 2050.
  • India has ratified the CBD and is one of 196 nations to have done so.
  • India formulated the National Biodiversity Strategy and Action Plan (NBSAP) 2024–2030 aligning with KMGBF, setting 23 national biodiversity targets.
  • India's Biological Diversity Act was enacted in 2002 (amended in 2023).
  • PSUs in India contribute about 14% to India's GDP.
  • NPC (National Productivity Council) was established in 1958.
  • NPC has been entrusted as an EADA (Environment Audit Designated Agency) since 18th March 2026.
  • Basel Convention deals with transboundary movements of hazardous wastes and their disposal.
  • Rotterdam Convention deals with Prior Informed Consent procedure for hazardous chemicals and pesticides in international trade.
  • Stockholm Convention deals with Persistent Organic Pollutants (POPs).
  • POPs include organochlorine pesticides such as DDT, industrial chemicals PCBs, and unintentional byproducts dioxins (PCDD) and furans (PCDF).
  • NTPC (National Thermal Power Corporation) released a biodiversity conservation policy in 2022 to achieve No Net Loss of Biodiversity.

⚖️ Laws, Acts & International Conventions

India
Wild Life (Protection) Act, 1972
Indian parliamentary act providing comprehensive legal framework for protection of wild animals, birds, and plants covering the entire country. Restricts hunting, regulates wildlife product trade, establishes national parks and sanctuaries.
India — Amendment
Wildlife (Protection) Amendment Act, 2022
Streamlined original 6 schedules into 4 categories: Schedule I (highest/endangered), II (general protection), III (protected plants), IV (CITES-listed). Implemented to align with CITES.
India
Biological Diversity Act, 2002 (amended 2023)
Aims to conserve biological resources, promote sustainable use, ensure fair benefit-sharing. Enacted for UN CBD obligations. Mandates NBA (2003, Chennai), SBBs, and BMCs.
India
Environmental Protection Act, 1986
Makes EIA mandatory for 29 categories of developmental activities with investments ≥ Rs. 50 crores. The governing instrument is EIA Notification, 2006.
International
Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD), 1992
Established in 1992 to promote conservation, sustainable use, and equitable sharing of genetic resources. 196 nations ratified. Aichi Targets (2011–2020): 20 goals; KMGBF adopted at COP 15 in 2022.
International
Basel Convention
International action on control of transboundary movements of hazardous wastes and their disposal.
International
Rotterdam Convention
Prior Informed Consent (PIC) procedure for certain hazardous chemicals and pesticides in international trade.
International
Stockholm Convention
Deals with Persistent Organic Pollutants (POPs) including DDT, PCBs, dioxins (PCDD), and furans (PCDF).

🌡️ Carbon & Climate — Key Statements

  • Replacing coal with gas or oil would reduce the carbon dioxide emission (carbon footprints).
  • Solar energy and wind energy have almost zero carbon dioxide emissions.
  • Using hydrogen energy, fuel cells, and cleaner electric energy would also reduce the carbon footprints.
  • A quad = 10¹⁵ BTU = 1.055 × 10¹⁸ joules = 1.055 exajoules (EJ).
  • Reference book: Introduction to Environmental Engineering and Science by Gilbert M. Masters, Prentice Hall of India, 1991.
  • Forests, wetlands, mangroves, and grasslands act as carbon sinks, reducing greenhouse gas concentrations.
  • Greenhouse gases primarily include carbon dioxide (CO₂), methane, and nitrous oxide.
  • CO₂ level in atmosphere in 2026: approximately 430 ppm.
  • Earth's surface temperature has risen by 1.5°C over 1800s baseline.
  • 2025 was among the top three warmest years on record.

🌍 Environmental Problems — Key Facts

Sociological Perspectives

  • Structural-Functionalist perspective raises awareness of latent dysfunctions — negative consequences of social actions that are unintended and not widely recognized.
  • Conflict perspective focuses on how wealth, power, and the pursuit of profit underlie environmental problems.
  • Symbolic Interactionist perspective is linked to Greenwashing and Pinkwashing.
  • Pinkwashing uses pink ribbons to suggest a company is helping fight breast cancer.
  • The relationship between economic development and environmental pollution is the Environmental Kuznets Curve (EKC) — an inverted U-shape.

Hazardous Wastes & Chemicals

  • POPs = Persistent Organic Pollutants, including DDT, PCBs, dioxins (PCDD), furans (PCDF).
  • Multiple Chemical Sensitivity (MCS) is also known as environmental illness.
  • PFAS = Per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances, known as "forever chemicals".
  • PTFE = Polytetrafluoroethylene (also known as TEFLON).
  • WTO, NAFTA, and FTAA are free trade agreements that weaken governments' ability to protect natural resources and implement environmental legislation.

Air & Water Pollution

  • Air pollutants include: carbon monoxide, sulfur dioxide, arsenic, nitrogen dioxide, mercury, dioxins, and lead.
  • Acid rain is caused when sulfur dioxide and nitrogen oxide mix with precipitation.
  • Dead zones in Gulf of Mexico, Chesapeake Bay, and Lake Erie have oxygen levels so low they cannot support life due to fertilizer pollution runoff.
  • Fracking = hydraulic fracturing, used in natural gas production by injecting water, sand, and chemicals into underground wells to break shale rock.
  • BS VI Norms relate to transportation vehicle emission standards.
  • Hydrofluoroolefins (HFOs) are the fourth generation of synthetic refrigerants, replacing CFCs.

Green Energy

  • Green energy / clean energy sources include solar, wind, biofuel, and hydrogen.
  • Biofuels are derived from agricultural crops. Two types: ethanol and biodiesel.
  • Maglev = magnetic levitation. Maximum speed: 603 km/h. Used in China, Japan, and Korea.
  • The Adam Joseph Lewis Center for Environmental Studies at Oberlin College, Ohio, USA is one of the most environmentally friendly buildings. Vision of David Orr.
